Safdarjung Tomb

The Safdarjung Tomb is a historical mausoleum located in New Delhi, India. It is one of the notable monuments in the city and represents a fine example of Mughal-style architecture.

Historical Significance:

The Safdarjung Tomb was built in 1754 as a mausoleum for Mirza Muqim Abul Mansur Khan, who was given the title "Safdarjung" by the Mughal Emperor Muhammad Shah. Safdarjung served as the Prime Minister of the Mughal Empire during the reign of Muhammad Shah.

Architectural Style:

The mausoleum is constructed in the late Mughal architectural style, which is characterized by a combination of Mughal and Persian elements. It's often compared to the Humayun's Tomb in Delhi and the Taj Mahal in Agra, as it shares similarities with these iconic structures.

Structure and Layout:

The Safdarjung Tomb is a sandstone and marble structure featuring a large central dome and four smaller domes at its corners. The central dome is surrounded by a beautiful garden, Mughal-style charbagh, which is divided into four squares by pathways and features fountains and ornamental plants.

Cultural and Tourist Attraction:

Efforts have been made to preserve and restore the Safdarjung Tomb over the years to ensure that its historical and architectural value is maintained.

Visiting Safdarjung Tomb:

The mausoleum is open to visitors, and it's advisable to check for the latest information on visiting hours and any entry fees when planning a visit.


The Safdarjung Tomb is not only a historical monument but also a place where visitors can appreciate the architectural beauty and historical significance of the Mughal era in India. It stands as a testament to the memory of Safdarjung, who served as a prominent figure in the Mughal court during a critical period in Indian history.
