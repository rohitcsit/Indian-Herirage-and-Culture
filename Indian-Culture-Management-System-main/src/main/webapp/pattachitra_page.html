Pattachitra

Pattachitra is a traditional and intricate art form that hails from the eastern Indian state of Odisha. This form of painting has a rich history and is characterized by its vibrant colors, intricate details, and the depiction of mythological and folk themes. The term "Pattachitra" is derived from the Sanskrit words "patta," meaning "cloth," and "chitra," meaning "picture," as the art is traditionally created on cloth.

Historical Significance:

Pattachitra has a long history, with its roots dating back to ancient times. It was traditionally practiced by skilled artists, known as "chitrakars," who created these intricate paintings as a form of storytelling, often depicting religious and mythological stories.

Materials and Technique:

Pattachitra is made using natural pigments, often handmade from stones, minerals, and plants. The artist grinds these pigments and mixes them with a binding agent, usually gum arabic, to create vibrant colors. The paintings are done on specially prepared cloth or palm leaves. The use of fine brushes, intricate line work, and detailed ornamentation are characteristic of this art form.

Themes and Motifs:

Pattachitra paintings typically depict themes from Hindu mythology and folklore, with popular subjects including the epics like the Ramayana and Mahabharata, scenes from the life of Lord Krishna, and various other deities. Nature, animals, and daily village life are also common motifs.

Regional Styles:

There are several regional variations within the Pattachitra tradition, including the Puri, Raghurajpur, and Kalahandi styles. Each style has its own unique features, including the choice of colors, brushwork, and artistic conventions.

Cultural Significance:

Pattachitra is deeply rooted in Odisha's cultural and religious traditions. These paintings are used in various festivals, rituals, and religious ceremonies, often as a means of storytelling and cultural expression. They serve both religious and artistic purposes and are considered highly auspicious.

Revival and Contemporary Adaptations:

In addition to the traditional form, Pattachitra has also seen contemporary adaptations, such as the use of new mediums like canvas and paper. Contemporary artists have explored new themes and styles while preserving the core characteristics of the art.

Global Appeal:

The vibrant colors, intricate details, and rich storytelling of Pattachitra have made it appealing to art enthusiasts both in India and around the world. These paintings continue to be highly sought after as decorative pieces, collectibles, and art forms that embody India's rich cultural heritage.


Pattachitra is not just a form of traditional art but also a powerful storytelling medium. It continues to captivate art connoisseurs and those interested in India's rich cultural and artistic traditions.
